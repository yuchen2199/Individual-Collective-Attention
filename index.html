Learning from Individual to Collective:
A Unified Framework for Driver-aware Attention Prediction

Yuchen Zhou, Zipeng Guo, Linkai Liu, Zipeng Guo, Chao Gou
Sun Yat-sen University
In submission

Individual-Collective Driver Attention Dataset (ICDA)

To address the gap in research on individual differences in driver attention, we introduce the novel Individual-Collective Driver Attention Benchmark tailored for the task of driver-aware attention prediction. This task aims to jointly predict both individual and collective driver attentions within identical driving contexts. Specifically, we develop the novel **I**ndividual-**C**ollective **D**river **A**ttention (ICDA) dataset. This dataset distinguishes between the attentions of individual drivers within the same driving scenarios, comprising data from 16 drivers across 370 traffic videos. It provides a robust foundation for training and evaluating models that need to understand nuanced individual behaviors alongside collective patterns.

Abstract

Driver attention prediction is a pivotal task for the advancement of Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) and Autonomous Vehicles (AV), garnering increasing scholarly attention and yielding significant progress. Existing studies primarily focus on predicting the attention of individual drivers or aggregate multiple drivers' attention patterns indiscriminately, studying only their collective attention. However, these methods often overlook the individual differences and visual preferences among drivers, which are crucial for personalized services and overall safety in ADAS and AV systems. To bridge this critical gap, we have reconceptualized the model designs, task settings, and dataset benchmarks. We introduce the Individual-Collective Attention Model (ICnet), a novel unified framework that effectively integrates individual and collective driver attention modeling. To encourage the model to capture both individual differences and similarities among drivers, we propose an adaptive attention training strategy. Furthermore, we establish a pioneering benchmark for multitask prediction of both individual and collective driver attention, termed driver-aware attention prediction. This benchmark leverages the first dataset that differentiates individual driver attention within identical contexts, comprising 16 drivers across 370 traffic videos. Through extensive quantitative and qualitative analyses, our ICnet outperforms existing methods in both tasks of individual and collective attention prediction, affirming the effectiveness and robustness of our approach.
